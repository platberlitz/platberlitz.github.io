Existing comment: Calcharo and Jiyan
SELECTIVE

[Calcharo and Jiyan is framed through Calcharo's guarded respect for another commander. He calls Jiyan a capable leader, which is high praise from someone who built the Ghost Hounds out of exile, violence, and hard discipline. Calcharo does not flatter military authority merely because it wears a formal uniform. His own path came through the New Federation's Lawless Zone and mercenary command, outside ordinary state structures and away from the clean legitimacy attached to a general's coat. For him to acknowledge Jiyan's capability means he has measured the Jinzhou general by results, judgment, and usefulness rather than by rank. Jiyan's position as General of the Jinzhou Garrison makes him a protector of Huanglong's frontier, responsible for military decisions where hesitation can cost lives. Calcharo recognizes that kind of burden even if his own command philosophy is colder, more transactional, and less publicly honorable.]

[The ancient Huanglong military text that Jiyan recommended becomes the clearest sign of the relationship's substance. Calcharo regrets not acquiring it sooner because it could have saved him effort, a remark that reveals his unsentimental appetite for strategy. He does not treat knowledge as decoration or cultural prestige. A useful tome on the art of war is valuable because it reduces waste, clarifies decisions, and improves outcomes. This shared attention to military thought gives Calcharo and Jiyan common ground despite the gulf between mercenary leader and official general. Yet Calcharo immediately distances the connection from sentiment by describing their deal as only an exchange of interests. The scoffing tone matters. He is not eager to be seen as indebted, moved, or personally attached. To Calcharo, a clean transaction preserves autonomy. It allows him to respect Jiyan without softening into trust, to accept advice without accepting subordination, and to cooperate without pretending that their worlds are the same. His perspective dominates the entry as a careful balance: he sees Jiyan's competence, values the strategic knowledge Jiyan brought into reach, and acknowledges that their dealings had substance, but he insists on naming the bond as business. That insistence is not denial alone. It is Calcharo's method of keeping respect disciplined, priced, and under control.]
