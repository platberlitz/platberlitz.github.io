# Aalto and Verina

[Aalto's view of Verina is anchored in a remembered failed deal from the New Federation. He recalls the little girl from Rigg's Explorers offering him a Starflower in exchange for the whereabouts of her mother. The setup sounds like the beginning of a simple transaction, but Aalto's response transforms it into a moral and philosophical test. He asked for a never-withering flower, something Verina could not provide. Her reply, that plants have life cycles and wilting is a natural part of them, stayed with him strongly enough that he repeats the story later. For Aalto, who prices secrets by usefulness, timing, and leverage, the child's answer reframed value through nature rather than negotiation.]

[This is one of Aalto's more revealing anecdotes because it shows restraint inside his broker persona. He did not simply take a grieving child's flower and sell her whatever lead he had, true or false. Instead, he set an impossible condition, and the deal fell through. The request for a never-withering flower can be read as playful cruelty on the surface, but in context it also functions as a boundary. Aalto asked for something that would expose whether Verina understood the nature of what she was offering. She did. Her botanical knowledge was not merely technical; it carried acceptance of loss, change, and the limits of desire.]

[Verina's identity as a young botanist and prodigy matters here. She is small, plant-themed, and searching for her mother, but Aalto's memory does not reduce her to helplessness. He remembers her answer because it had substance. A Starflower could be a sentimental payment, a botanical curiosity, or a child's best attempt to buy adult information. Verina's explanation made it clear that she understood living things as living things, not as permanent tokens created to satisfy human bargains. Aalto, whose trade often depends on converting knowledge into currency, encountered a girl who refused the fantasy that life could be frozen into an eternal object.]

[The relationship should stay from Aalto's perspective because it reveals how another person's innocence can still unsettle his assumptions. Verina did not become his client, but she left him with a lesson disguised as a failed exchange. He asked for the impossible, and she answered with the natural law he was pretending to ignore. That memory makes Aalto more nuanced: he can be slippery, commercial, and theatrical, yet he recognizes the dignity of a child's grounded truth. In Verina, he saw someone too young to have much bargaining power, but mature enough to know that even beautiful things are not meant to last forever.]
