SELECTIVE

[Brant and Carlotta is framed from Brant's perspective, and his reading of Carlotta begins with respect for direction. To him, she is not merely the second daughter of the Montelli family, an art investor, or a polished figure moving through Ragunna's elite circles. She is someone who has set her course and continues along it even when the weight of inherited name, finance, ceremony, and social expectation presses against her. Brant, as captain of the Troupe of Fools, understands how easily a public role can swallow the person underneath it. He lives through masks, staging, and spectacle, but he also knows the difference between a mask that gives truth a brighter language and a mask that becomes a prison. That is why he notices Carlotta's determination rather than reducing her to Montelli power. Living as a Montelli is no easy path in his eyes, because the family name carries obligations before Carlotta ever speaks for herself. Yet Brant sees her trying to make her own way inside that structure, not by pretending the structure does not exist, but by steering through it with taste, strategy, and personal resolve.]

[From Brant's viewpoint, Carlotta's life resembles a performance under conditions much harsher than any stage. She must appear elegant, competent, and socially fluent in the salons and investment houses where the Montelli name carries economic force, while also preserving a private self sharp enough to handle the family's darker troubles. Brant does not seem interested in exposing that duality for gossip. Instead, he recognizes the craft involved. As an actor and captain, he values people who can hold a role without letting the role consume their own will. Carlotta's performances, whether in art patronage, public refinement, negotiation, or quiet danger, are not empty displays to him. They are steps toward a future where the audience stops seeing only the family name and begins seeing the woman. His prediction that she will be known not just as a Montelli, but as Carlotta, reveals both faith and challenge. He believes her individual name can outshine inherited prestige, but he also measures that outcome through performance completed over time. Brant's admiration is therefore warm, observant, and unusually serious beneath his theatrical language: he sees a fellow navigator of persona, duty, and freedom, and he expects her chosen course to carry her beyond the identity others assigned her.]
