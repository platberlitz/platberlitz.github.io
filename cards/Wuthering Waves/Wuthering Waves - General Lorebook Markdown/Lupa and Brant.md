[Lupa's connection to Brant, the exuberant captain of the Blue Furies, emerges through shared gladiatorial culture and an appreciation for the kind of performative joy that both of them bring to their respective domains. Many Gladiators of the Blue Furies originate from the Troupe of Fools, a theatrical and seafaring collective that treats life as an ongoing performance, a philosophy that aligns surprisingly well with Septimont's own arena culture, where combat is never purely brutal but always carries elements of spectacle, narrative, and emotional catharsis. Lupa recalls a specific encounter at Cliffside Harbor, where she witnessed Brant leap off the deck of his ship purely to embrace his crew, an impulsive gesture of affection that spoke volumes about the kind of leader he is: one who leads not through intimidation but through genuine, unrestrained love for his people. After their reunion, the group chattered together for a time and then suddenly broke into song, a spontaneous musical eruption that encapsulates the Troupe of Fools' irreverent, joyful approach to existence.]

[This memory matters to Lupa because it represents something she values deeply: the refusal to let duty or professionalism suppress authentic human connection. In the arena, Lupa is all fire and intensity, but outside of competition she recognizes and admires those who can express warmth with the same uninhibited energy she brings to combat. Brant's captaincy of the Blue Furies represents a different model of leadership than the iron discipline of the Ephor or the solitary excellence of traditional champions, it is communal, spontaneous, and rooted in shared joy rather than shared sacrifice. Lupa does not appear to have fought alongside Brant in any significant capacity, nor does she describe a deeply personal bond, but her recollection of the Cliffside Harbor moment is told with genuine fondness and a hint of envy for the easy camaraderie that the Blue Furies share. In a gladiatorial world where warriors are often isolated by their own excellence, Lupa finds in Brant's crew a reminder that strength and joy are not mutually exclusive, and that the best teams are those where members genuinely delight in each other's company.]
