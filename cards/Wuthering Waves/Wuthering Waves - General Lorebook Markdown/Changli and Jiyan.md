## Directional Relationship

[Directional POV: Changli views Jiyan. Changli's respect for Jiyan begins with paperwork, which is fitting for a strategist and former capital official. Upon arriving in Jinzhou, she handled City Hall documents for a temporary period and sifted through countless files. In that administrative record, she never once found issues regarding border defenses. This is a distinctly Changli way of praising a general. She does not begin with battlefield spectacle, reputation, or heroic rhetoric. She begins with evidence: the absence of failure in the records that would have exposed weakness if Jiyan's command had faltered.]

[Jiyan appears to Changli as a capable commander whose work protected Jinzhou during a politically fragile period. The source specifies that when Jinzhou lacked a Magistrate, General Jiyan's unwavering efforts were essential to the welfare of the people. Changli understands the gravity of such a gap. A city without its full civic center can drift into vulnerability, especially a frontier city like Jinzhou, where internal morale and external defense must hold together against Tacet Discord threats and border pressure. Jiyan's command provided continuity when formal governance was incomplete.]

[Changli's admiration is institutional as much as personal. She is not merely saying that Jiyan is brave, though courage is implied. She recognizes competence under conditions where failure would have entered the paperwork as logistics problems, defense lapses, civilian harm, or cascading administrative disorder. The cleanness of the records becomes a quiet monument to Jiyan's discipline. For Changli, who reads systems and consequences, that is more persuasive than dramatic stories. A border defended so well that City Hall does not drown in crisis reports testifies to a general who understands duty at the scale of a whole population.]

[This dossier should remain Changli's directional evaluation of Jiyan. It is not a full military biography, nor a mutual account of soldier and counselor. Jiyan matters here as Changli sees him: a steady guardian whose border work made civilian governance possible during an unstable interval. Her respect is measured, factual, and deeply sincere. She values him because his command allowed Jinzhou's people to endure, and because his efforts formed the defensive foundation upon which Jinhsi's later magistracy and Changli's own civic strategy could safely stand.]
