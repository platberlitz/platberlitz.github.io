Existing comment: Cartethyia and Ciaccona
SELECTIVE

[Cartethyia's relationship with Ciaccona is rooted in childhood memory and the power of art to transcend time, creating a connection that exists more in the realm of emotional resonance than direct interaction. When Cartethyia was a child, before her elevation to Blessed Maiden and before the full weight of her dual nature became apparent, she used to beg bards passing through town to sing her stories of knights, tales of heroism, honor, and the kind of straightforward virtue that her own complicated existence would later make impossible to embody. These stories represented something pure and aspirational, a world where good and evil were clearly defined, where knights fought for justice and won through courage rather than through cosmic curses and divine manipulation. This childhood fascination with knightly tales reveals a side of Cartethyia that remains fundamentally romantic and idealistic despite everything she has endured, someone who still responds to narratives of heroism even after becoming far more complex than any hero she once imagined herself to be. The memory of those begging moments, of a young girl eager for tales of adventure, stands in stark contrast to the woman she would become, someone whose own story is far darker and more ambiguous than any bard's song could capture.]

[The pivotal moment in their connection occurred after Cartethyia was freed from the tower, a detail that suggests she was imprisoned or confined at some point, likely due to the dangerous nature of her Threnodian power or as part of her duties as Blessed Maiden. Wandering through a field one day, experiencing the simple freedom of movement that most people take for granted, she heard the wind carrying a piece of beautiful music to her ears. Following the sound, she stood beneath a tree in the distance and listened as Ciaccona sang a new poem, one that incorporated the melody of Egla Town's folk songs, traditional music that connected to the common people rather than to sacred ceremony or aristocratic entertainment. The experience was so captivating that in the blink of an eye, noon had already given way to dusk, seven hours lost in the simple act of listening to someone sing. This anecdote reveals the profound impact that art and beauty have on Cartethyia, how music can temporarily free her from the burdens she carries and allow her to simply exist as a listener rather than as a vessel, a weapon, or a symbol. For Ciaccona, this moment likely represents one of the most meaningful performances of her life, not because of the size of the audience or the grandeur of the venue, but because of who was listening. To hold the attention of the Blessed Maiden for seven hours, to make someone who carries cosmic powers forget her burdens long enough for an entire afternoon to vanish, is an achievement that speaks to the genuine power of Ciaccona's art. Their relationship thus exists primarily through the medium of performance, with Ciaccona's music serving as a bridge between Cartethyia's isolated divine existence and the ordinary human world of folk songs, traveling bards, and stories that have nothing to do with saving or destroying the world. It is a connection built on the recognition that beauty matters even in the face of apocalypse, that art can provide temporary sanctuary from impossible burdens, and that sometimes the most profound relationships are expressed not through words or actions but through the simple act of singing while someone else listens.]
