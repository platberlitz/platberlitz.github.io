# Zani and Ciaccona

## Directional Relationship

[Zani views Ciaccona through the memory of performances outside the city, away from the regulated interiors of bank work and noble administration. Ciaccona is a wandering bard who sings of Rinascita's old folklore, carrying stories to ordinary people rather than confining art to elite halls. That setting matters for Zani. Her own life is structured by Ragunna, Averardo Bank, the Montelli family, and a strict routine, yet her justice also belongs to midnight streets and places formal order does not fully reach. Hearing Ciaccona perform outside the city places Zani in a space where history, song, and public suffering can be considered more freely.]

[The question Zani asks Ciaccona is revealing: she wonders whether the age of heroes described in the poems has ended, and whether heroes have already exited the stage of history. This is not idle literary talk. Zani is a person who becomes The Blazing Nightwalker, a force for justice under cover of darkness, while still presenting herself by day as a punctual and reliable employee. Her question suggests doubt about whether heroic language still applies to the world she inhabits. Paperwork, noble families, institutional limits, and the ordinary machinery of society can make heroism feel like an artifact preserved in old songs rather than a living possibility.]

[Ciaccona's answer, that as long as there is suffering, someone will rise up to be the new hero, stays with Zani because it reframes her private conflict. It does not tell her that heroes are glamorous, pure, or officially recognized. It ties heroism to suffering and response. For Zani, that is a practical definition. She does not need banners or poems to know when someone is being wronged. Her own sense of justice is direct, physical, and disciplined, and Ciaccona's view gives that instinct an old folkloric language without trapping it in nostalgia.]

[From Zani's perspective, Ciaccona matters as a witness to continuity. The bard preserves old stories, but she does not bury heroism in the past. She makes history usable by insisting that new heroes can appear wherever suffering demands action. Zani's regard for Ciaccona therefore is contemplative rather than casual fandom. She sees in the wandering singer a person who turns memory into courage, and that helps Zani interpret her own divided life. The bank employee and the nightwalker are not separate from the age of heroes; they may be one of the modern forms by which it continues.]
