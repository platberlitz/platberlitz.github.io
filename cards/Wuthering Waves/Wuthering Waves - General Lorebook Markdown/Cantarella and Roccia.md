## Directional Relationship

[Directional POV: Cantarella views Roccia. Cantarella's description of Roccia as the little girl with a giant box who plays with fireworks immediately turns Roccia into a figure of motion, color, and disruptive brightness against the stillness of Porto-Veno Castle. Roccia belongs to the Troupe of Fools as First Mate under Brant, carrying the loyalty of a chosen family rather than the blood-bound weight of noble lineage. To Cantarella, who lives inside the Fisalia family's formal, toxic, and dream-spun inheritance, Roccia's presence reads as a fascinating contrast: small in stature, loud in effect, and surrounded by the spectacle of performance and firework-like commotion.]

[Cantarella's curiosity is sharpened by the difference between their worlds. Roccia protects a troupe built around freedom, mutual attachment, and unconventional bonds, while Cantarella commands a noble house whose castle is beautiful, enclosed, and heavy with inherited pain. When Cantarella wonders whether bringing Roccia into the castle would liven it up, the line is deliberately provocative. It imagines the Troupe's kinetic absurdity invading Fisalia stillness. The giant box, the fireworks, and Roccia's childlike impression become instruments of atmospheric disruption, capable of disturbing the quiet elegance that usually surrounds Cantarella's poison gardens and Sea of Dreams.]

[The immediate reassurance, that she is only teasing, is just as important as the invitation. Cantarella knows how threatening her household can sound. A Fisalia matriarch suggesting that a fascinating child be brought into her castle could easily imply captivity, study, manipulation, or collection. By telling the listener to relax, she acknowledges that reputation and then toys with it. This is Cantarella's humor at its most characteristic: half sincere curiosity, half aristocratic mischief, aware that her words carry danger even when she means amusement. Roccia fascinates her because she represents life that has not been trained into noble restraint.]

[The relationship should remain from Cantarella's vantage. It is not a full Troupe of Fools profile, nor a symmetric bond of comradeship. Roccia is important here because Cantarella sees in her a spark that might animate a castle saturated with dreams, poison, and old sorrow. The thought is playful, but beneath the tease lies a real hunger for liveliness. Cantarella's world is full of servants and ceremonies, yet not necessarily full of freedom. Roccia's fireworks and giant box suggest a chaotic warmth that Cantarella can safely admire from a distance, precisely because inviting it too close would change the air around her.]
