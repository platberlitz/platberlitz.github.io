Existing comment: Mornye and Luuk Herssen
SELECTIVE

[Mornye views Luuk Herssen through a memory from her student years, when she frequented the infirmary and became the target of one of Dr. Herssen's most effective benevolent deceptions. At the time, Luuk told her to take a medicine imported from the New Federation, with the condition that it would only take effect if she went to bed before 10 P.M. The instruction was medically theatrical, but perfectly suited to Mornye's likely problem. As a brilliant student already inclined toward intense study and self-neglect, she may not have obeyed a simple request to rest. A prescription, however, gave rest the authority of treatment.]

[A month passed and her health improved. From Mornye's perspective, the improvement is important because it proves the intervention worked before she understood its mechanism. Only later did she learn that the medicines were ordinary Lahai-Roi plant-based milk. The revelation turns Luuk's treatment into a lesson about care, trust, and the body. He had not cured her with a special Federation drug. He had altered her behavior by giving her a ritual she would follow, and that ritual forced her to sleep at a humane hour. Mornye, who spends her adult life mapping profound structures of space-time and teaching Exostrider Engineering, is likely capable of appreciating the elegance of the trick even if she also recognizes the deception.]

[The relationship is therefore affectionate in a quietly ironic way. Luuk's past is described as one of blood and lies, yet here he uses a lie to reveal a practical truth: Mornye needed rest more than exotic medicine. His role as Startorch Academy's doctor and counselor is not just to dispense treatment, but to understand the person refusing to live within healthy limits. Mornye's memory preserves him as a physician who knew how to make a stubborn student cooperate without humiliating her. It also shows the continuity between her student life and professorial life. Before Mornye became a senior researcher and professor, she was a young person in the infirmary, vulnerable enough to be helped by a cup of milk disguised as imported medicine. Her view of Luuk carries gratitude, dry amusement, and respect for a healer whose methods were unconventional but effective.]
