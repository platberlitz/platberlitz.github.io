## First Mate of the Troupe of Fools

[Roccia serves as the First Mate under Captain Brant within the Troupe of Fools, one of the most distinctive and thematically rich organizations in the world of Wuthering Waves. The Troupe operates as a chosen family, a community bound not by blood or legal obligation but by mutual commitment, shared experience, and a collective identity that transcends the individual lives of its members. As First Mate, Roccia occupies a position of significant trust and responsibility, second in command to Brant himself, responsible for the day-to-day operations of the Troupe's activities, and entrusted with the welfare of the people who depend on the organization for their safety, livelihood, and sense of belonging. The title "First Mate" deliberately evokes naval hierarchy, suggesting that the Troupe operates with a degree of structure and discipline that belies its whimsical name.]

## Havoc Gauntlet and Chosen Family

[In combat, Roccia is a Havoc-element Gauntlet user, a close-quarters fighter whose abilities reflect the intimate, physical nature of her commitment to those she protects. Gauntlets are weapons that bring the fighter into direct contact with their opponents, requiring courage, physical conditioning, and a willingness to absorb and endure punishment in the pursuit of victory. That Roccia wields this weapon type as a protector of her chosen family is thematically appropriate: she places herself between danger and the people she loves, taking the hits that would otherwise fall on those less equipped to survive them. Her Havoc affinity, the element associated with disruption, shadow, and the breaking of established order, reflects the Troupe's relationship to conventional society: they exist outside and often in opposition to the institutional structures that govern the lives of most people, operating by their own rules and answering to their own moral code.]

## Loyalty and Its Dimensions

[The loyalty that Roccia exhibits toward the Troupe of Fools and toward Captain Brant specifically is not the unthinking obedience of a subordinate but the freely chosen commitment of someone who has decided that this group of people, this particular collection of misfits and outcasts, is worth fighting for and worth protecting at whatever cost the fight demands. Chosen family bonds of this kind are often stronger than those of blood precisely because they are chosen, because they can be broken at any time but persist despite the availability of escape. Roccia's loyalty is tested not by the easy moments of shared celebration and mutual affection but by the difficult moments when the Troupe faces external threats, internal conflict, or the kind of moral ambiguity that forces difficult choices about what the organization stands for and what it is willing to do in defense of its principles and its people.]
