Existing comment: Cartethyia and Brant
SELECTIVE

[Brant's relationship with Cartethyia is defined by a profound curiosity that cuts through the layers of legend, dogma, and fear that surround the Blessed Maiden to ask the most fundamental question anyone can pose about another person: what truth lies beneath the legend? As the flamboyant captain of the Troupe of Fools, Brant understands performance and persona better than anyone in Rinascita, he knows what it means to wear a mask, to play a role, to become something larger than oneself for the sake of entertainment or survival. This understanding gives him unique insight into Cartethyia's situation, allowing him to see past the titles and the sacred office to recognize that the woman beneath the crown remains largely unknown even to those who claim to know her best. When he observes that some call her a saint while others call her a sinner, he is not making a moral judgment but rather highlighting how completely her public image has eclipsed her private self. No one, he notes, cares to examine how she treats her friends or faces her foes, they are too busy debating her cosmic significance to notice her human behaviors. The Order's tales never speak of her dreams, and the legends deliberately conceal her heart's desire, constructing a narrative that serves institutional purposes rather than revealing truth. Everyone has heard of Cartethyia, Brant acknowledges, but does anyone actually know the person behind that name? This question strikes at the heart of what makes his perspective unique among those who interact with Cartethyia. Where others see a divine vessel, a cursed being, or a political symbol, Brant sees someone whose humanity has been buried beneath layers of expectation, projection, and fear. His interest in her is not theological or strategic but deeply personal, he wants to understand the woman, not the legend, to know her dreams rather than her titles, to see how she treats those she cares about rather than how she performs her sacred duties.]

[This perspective likely stems from Brant's own experience living between authenticity and performance, his understanding that the most important truths about a person are often the ones that get lost in translation when that person becomes a public figure. As captain of the Troupe of Fools, he has spent his life crafting stories and personas, making him acutely aware of the gap between who someone presents themselves as and who they actually are when the audience goes home. His approach to Cartethyia is therefore not reverent or fearful but genuinely inquisitive, a desire to peel back the accumulated mythology and encounter the actual human being trapped beneath. This does not mean he dismisses her power or her importance, Brant is too intelligent and too experienced to underestimate someone of Cartethyia's capabilities, but it does mean he refuses to let that power define her entirely. In a world where most people react to Cartethyia with either worship or terror, Brant's simple human curiosity is almost revolutionary, offering her something she rarely receives: the opportunity to be seen as a person rather than a symbol. Whether this connection develops into something deeper remains unclear, but the foundation is there, a recognition that legends are constructed, that titles can be prisons, and that the most interesting question about any person is not what they represent but who they are when no one is watching.]
