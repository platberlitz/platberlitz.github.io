Existing comment: Phrolova and Cristoforo
SELECTIVE

[Phrolova and Cristoforo: Phrolova views Cristoforo: Phrolova regards Cristoforo's pursuit of true free will as intriguing because it touches the same fault line that runs through her own existence. Cristoforo asks whether mankind can awaken a will capable of confronting the truths of the world rather than merely obeying fate, prophecy, resonance patterns, or institutional scripts. Phrolova, as a former Fractsidus Overseer and a Resonator whose music can tune the frequencies of beings, understands the temptation of that question. She has seen how easily people become notes inside larger arrangements. She has also built her life around the possibility that melodies can be changed, even when life and death seem to have fixed the score.]

[Her interest is not approval. Phrolova pities the weakness she sees in Cristoforo's premise: blind will without the anchor of survival becomes a flash in the pan. This judgment is distinctly hers. She has not survived by faith in abstract agency. She survived death, grief, betrayal, and the collapse of a past she wants to reclaim. To her, will is not meaningful unless it endures pressure, cost, hunger, fear, and the world's refusal to cooperate. A philosophy that encourages mankind to confront truth may sound noble, but Phrolova listens for the missing bass note. If free will cannot preserve the one who exercises it, then it becomes a brief flare of defiance swallowed by the same darkness it opposed.]

[The warning bell she hears in uncontrollable turns and imperfect endings reveals how she interprets Cristoforo's project. He may see gaps in the script as proof that agency can break determinism. Phrolova sees those cracks as signs of instability, places where a composition loses structure and risks collapse. Her own methods are theatrical and controlled, despite their horror. She values arrangement, timing, and the ability to conduct chaos into form. Cristoforo's free will interests her because it seeks a truth she cannot dismiss, but she measures it against survival and finds it incomplete. From Phrolova's perspective, mankind's will must do more than rebel against fate. It must keep singing after fate strikes back.]
