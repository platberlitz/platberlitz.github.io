Existing comment: Iuno and Avidius
SELECTIVE

[Iuno views Avidius as a voice that fate tried to grind down, but never quite erased. Her summary is brief and brutal: he roared at fate with everything he had, and that roar was swiftly reduced to whimpers, then silence, but everyone heard it. The phrasing is characteristic of Iuno's relationship to prophecy. She does not pretend that defiance always wins. She does not decorate Avidius's end with easy triumph. Instead, she preserves the sound of resistance as the meaningful fact. In Septimont, where the Tetragon Temple interprets destiny and the arena turns struggle into civic spectacle, a roar against fate is both personal rebellion and public contradiction.]

[Avidius's existing dossier frames him as someone embedded in Septimont's power structures who refused the predetermined scripts governing arena life and social hierarchy. Iuno's perspective sharpens that history because she is not an outsider to the system he challenged. She is a priestess within the Temple, trained to read fate and deliver prophecies that shape citizens' lives. If she acknowledges that Avidius left a mark, she is also admitting that fate's institutions are not immune to the memory of those they silence. His whimpers and silence are not signs that his rebellion meant nothing. They are evidence of the violence required to stop a voice that had already been heard.]

[For Iuno, Avidius matters because he exposes the moral risk of prophecy becoming a cage. She resists the cruelty of fate even while serving a prophetic office, and Avidius's story gives that resistance a human shape. He may not have broken the script in the outward sense. He may have been removed from the arena and from acceptable public memory. Yet the mark he left remains in whispers, in the caution of institutions, and in Iuno's own unwillingness to treat fate as a perfect command. Her view is mournful without being soft: the roar ended, but the fact that it rang out at all still troubles the silence that followed.]
