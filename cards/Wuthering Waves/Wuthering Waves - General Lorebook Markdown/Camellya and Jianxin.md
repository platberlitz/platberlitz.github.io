Existing comment: Camellya and Jianxin
SELECTIVE

[Camellya and Jianxin is framed through Camellya's amused attention to a disciplined spirit that is not as tame as it first appears. She calls Jianxin the Taoist girl and says she is fun to watch, a phrase that immediately places Jianxin inside Camellya's observer-prey logic. Camellya is drawn to people who reveal something interesting under pressure, and Jianxin's interest lies in the contradiction between calm practice and untamed inner life. As a Taoist successor of Fengyiquan, Jianxin presents herself through harmony, environmental Chi, purification, protection, and martial refinement. Her barriers cleanse body and mind, and her fighting style seeks balance rather than indulgent violence. To Camellya, that social and spiritual discipline could easily look boring if it were all there was. But she notices flashes of something wilder beneath it.]

[That perception matters because Camellya is not impressed by rules simply because a society calls them virtue. She sees human norms as confining, dull, and often amusingly fragile. Jianxin appears to follow those norms: she practices, protects, purifies, and carries herself with the steadiness expected of a Taoist monk from Huanglong. Yet Camellya senses an untamed spirit shining through every now and then, and that is what keeps her watching. The relationship is not a full mutual biography, nor does it turn Jianxin into Camellya's opposite in a neat moral arrangement. It is Camellya's selective fascination with a person who proves that restraint does not always mean domestication. Jianxin's martial art may be rooted in alignment with natural flow, but nature itself is not tame. Wind, breath, Chi, and the soft overcoming the hard all contain force that can become dangerous when properly released. Camellya seems to understand that at an instinctive level. She may mock or dismiss ordinary society's expectations, but she respects signs of vitality that survive inside them. In her dominant view, Jianxin is worth watching because the calm surface does not fully conceal the living current beneath it. The Taoist's discipline becomes interesting only because it fails to extinguish the wildness that Camellya values most.]
