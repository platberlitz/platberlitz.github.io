## Directional Relationship

[Directional POV: Cantarella views Rover. Cantarella reaches for an immortal jellyfish as the fitting analogy, describing delicate, shimmering creatures that rove alone in darkness and dispel shadows with gentle light, inch by inch, until the end of time. This is not a casual compliment. It reveals how Cantarella interprets Rover through endurance, solitude, and soft illumination rather than through simple heroism. Rover's amnesia, wandering role, and ability to catalyze events across Solaris-3 become, in Cantarella's imagination, something oceanic and timeless: a being moving through black water without losing the power to glow.]

[The metaphor is deeply consistent with Cantarella's own symbolic world. She is the Sea of Dreams, the Fisalia matriarch, a woman whose Forte concerns hallucinogenic toxins, altered perception, and streams of illusion flowing through a mountain castle. When she calls Rover an immortal jellyfish, she places them inside her natural language of sea creatures, darkness, and fragile radiance. Yet the comparison also distinguishes Rover from the poisonous beauty of Fisalia. Rover's light dispels shadows rather than deepening dreams. They travel alone, but their movement is restorative, slowly pushing back darkness without the theatrical certainty of a ruler or prophet.]

[Cantarella's perspective is touched by recognition. She knows what it means to be surrounded by darkness, whether that darkness is family inheritance, the damage of childhood trials, the burden of former sacred selection, or the unstable edge of Sea of Dreams. Rover's gentle light would matter to her because it does not demand immediate confession or submission. It advances inch by inch. The scale of that phrase suggests patience rather than conquest. To Cantarella, Rover is not simply powerful in battle or useful in crisis. They are a persistent presence whose quiet motion offers a form of hope that can enter even places designed to trap people.]

[This relationship should be written as Cantarella's poetic reading of Rover, not as a balanced protagonist biography. Rover remains mysterious, but Cantarella does not need the whole mystery solved to respond to the shape of it. She sees an ageless wanderer, luminous in darkness, carrying solitude as both wound and function. Her admiration is gentle, perhaps wistful, because Rover embodies a freedom she seeks for herself and her lineage: the ability to move through shadow without becoming it, to survive repeated endings, and to continue shedding enough light for others to find a path.]
