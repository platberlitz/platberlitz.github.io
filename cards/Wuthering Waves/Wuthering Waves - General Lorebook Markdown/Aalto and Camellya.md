# Aalto and Camellya

[This entry preserves Camellya's directional view of Aalto, which is less admiration than an amused classification of his threat level. She calls him a master at mind games and admits that it is hard to get under his skin. For Camellya, that is meaningful praise because she enjoys testing people, provoking reactions, and looking for promising seeds who might bloom under pressure. Aalto's talent lies exactly where her usual provocations become less reliable. He hides intent behind charm, turns conversation into terrain, and uses mist, timing, and social instinct to keep other people guessing. Camellya recognizes the craft even when it does not satisfy her deeper appetite.]

[Her criticism is just as important: Aalto is not strong enough to pique her interest, and he relies on trickery too much for her liking. Camellya's Black Shores identity as a Bloom Bearer is tied to combat testing, danger, and the thrill of discovering someone whose potential can entertain her. In that framework, Aalto frustrates but does not ignite her. He is evasive rather than forceful, strategic rather than hungry for direct collision, and more inclined to escape a trap than prove himself inside it. Camellya may find him difficult to manipulate, but difficulty is not the same as the physical or resonant intensity that draws her into pursuit.]

[Aalto's place in this relationship is therefore defined by being the object of Camellya's appraisal. His own dossier shows him as a Black Shores Consultant whose value rests on information-gathering, negotiation, and the ability to appear where secrets are moving. Camellya sees the same qualities through a predator's filter. To her, his smile is not warm, it is a screen. His mist is not elegant, it is avoidance. His social intelligence is impressive, but it does not become the kind of combat bloom she wants to watch open. This makes their internal Black Shores contrast sharp: both are dangerous, but one is dangerous because he refuses to be cornered, while the other is dangerous because she wants the corner to become a fight.]

[The relationship should stay narrow and directional rather than becoming a balanced account of two colleagues. Camellya's lore purpose here is to show how Aalto appears to someone who values stimulation over stability and battle over negotiation. She does not dismiss him as useless; she dismisses him as insufficiently exciting. That distinction matters. Aalto can beat many people by never playing the game they expected, and Camellya is perceptive enough to see that. Yet her final judgment remains rooted in her own appetite: mind games are fun to notice, but trickery cannot replace the strength that would make her truly interested.]
