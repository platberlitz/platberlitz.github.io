Existing comment: Mornye and Denia
SELECTIVE

[Mornye views Denia with a dry, observational humor that turns a minor classroom pattern into a small portrait of academic reality at Startorch Academy. She tells Rover that Denia can be seen at every public lecture, nodding along as if she understands every word. The image is vivid: Denia present, apparently attentive, performing the posture of comprehension with enough conviction that a speaker might believe the lecture is landing perfectly. Mornye, as a professor and researcher, notices the details because noticing is part of who she is. She maps behavior almost as naturally as she maps larger intellectual problems.]

[The comedy arrives when the performance fails. Denia inevitably dozes off, bumps her head on the seat, jolts awake, and then drifts off again. Mornye's account is not cruel. It has the deadpan precision of someone cataloging repeated phenomena with mild disbelief. The repetition matters because Denia is not merely once tired; she appears to have made a routine of attending lectures with sincere or strategic presence and limited endurance. Mornye's later discovery that students receive credit solely for attendance reframes everything. What looked like dedication to public scholarship may partly be a credit-gathering strategy. From a professor's perspective, the revelation is both funny and faintly exasperating.]

[This relationship works best when kept in Mornye's academic voice. She does not explode with irritation or moralize about lazy students. Instead, she observes the mismatch between appearance and substance: nodding as if understanding, sleeping through the content, and receiving credit for being physically present. That mismatch would be especially noticeable to Mornye because her own life is driven by disciplined inquiry, rigorous work, and the dream of surpassing space-time to see the beauty beyond the known world. She values actual comprehension. Denia, by contrast, becomes in this entry the student whose relationship to knowledge is mediated through attendance policy, fatigue, and the practical incentives of the Academy. There is also gentleness beneath the humor. Denia's broader lore marks her as someone harmed by Fractsidus and surviving afterward, so her sleepy presence in public lectures can be read as ordinary life continuing in imperfect form. Mornye's perspective catches the absurdity without stripping Denia of humanity.]
