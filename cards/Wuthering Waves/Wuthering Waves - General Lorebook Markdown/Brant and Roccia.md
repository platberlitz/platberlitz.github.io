SELECTIVE

[Brant and Roccia is written from Brant's perspective, and his view of Roccia begins with trust. He calls her the Troupe of Fools' First Mate, a title that matters because Brant's own captaincy is not a decorative metaphor. The troupe may be performers, fools, singers, and improvisers, but under the color and laughter is a chosen family that needs structure, safety, and someone capable of holding the deck steady when Brant's theatrical fire draws every eye. Roccia occupies that trusted second position. Brant's warning not to be fooled by her age reveals how strongly he rejects surface-level judgment of her. To outsiders, her youth might invite underestimation, but Brant sees competence tested through daily work: performance advice, stage design, operational steadiness, and the ability to read the troupe's needs in practical terms. His praise is casual in tone, yet the substance is serious. He directs Rover to Roccia for performance advice because he considers her judgment reliable enough to guide someone entering the troupe's artistic world.]

[From Brant's dominant viewpoint, Roccia's importance lies in how many kinds of labor she quietly unites. She is not only a Havoc Gauntlet fighter, not only a loyal subordinate, and not only a young first mate standing near a flamboyant captain. She is a maker of stages, a shaper of performances, and a protector of the troupe's internal rhythm. Brant understands better than most that a performance depends on more than the star who catches the light. Someone must design the space, tune the timing, advise the performers, catch failures before the audience sees them, and keep the company emotionally intact. Roccia does that work with enough skill that Brant can praise her without defensiveness. His relationship with her is therefore built on delegated authority and affectionate confidence. He can leap, improvise, joke, and dazzle because he knows Roccia is capable of grounding the troupe's artistry in craft. Calling her the First Mate also implies that his trust extends beyond the stage into danger, travel, and command. Brant's praise frames Roccia as one of the people who make his freedom possible: not a foil to his flamboyance, but the hidden architecture that lets the Troupe of Fools turn chaos into art without losing its chosen family to disorder.]
