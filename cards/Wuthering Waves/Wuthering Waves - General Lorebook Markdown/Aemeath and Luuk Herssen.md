# Aemeath and Luuk Herssen

[Aemeath views Luuk Herssen as someone whose present kindness is real, but not simple. Her source comment begins with the public truth: Dr. Luuk is kind and competent, admired by many students. As head physician and counselor of Startorch Academy's Resonator Nursing Unit, Luuk occupies exactly the kind of role that students would associate with safety, treatment, and quiet reassurance. Aemeath recognizes that reputation. Yet she immediately adds that he was not always this way. The shift from public praise to private memory makes her perspective valuable, because Aemeath sees not only the doctor everyone trusts now, but the man who used to carry himself as if danger were always about to arrive.]

[Her image of the earlier Luuk is striking: a constant tension, as if bracing for battle at any moment. Aemeath's own status as a digital ghost gives this observation unusual intimacy. She can linger near people who may not perceive her, watching changes in posture, breath, habit, and emotional weather over time. Luuk's official dossier already suggests a mysterious past of blood and lies, but Aemeath does not need the full confession to notice its residue. She reads it in his body. Before he fully settled into the warmth of the Academy doctor, he looked like someone prepared for impact, perhaps even in peaceful rooms.]

[Aemeath wonders whether the change came when Rover invited Luuk to Lahai-Roi. That phrasing keeps the cause tentative rather than absolute, and the tentativeness suits her. She is not diagnosing him from authority; she is assembling meaning from what she witnessed. Rover's presence appears in several Lahai-Roi relationships as a catalyst for relief, change, or renewed connection. In Luuk's case, Aemeath sees a man who gradually stopped bracing so hard. The kindness admired by students may have existed before, but it became easier to inhabit after some burden shifted. To Aemeath, that transformation matters because it proves that even people shaped by violence can become gentler in visible ways.]

[This relationship should stay from Aemeath's perspective because she is the witness of Luuk's before and after. The dossier is not a full account of Luuk's past, nor a balanced summary of their mutual care. It is Aemeath's quiet recognition that the beloved doctor had to become what others now assume he always was. Her view gives him depth without prying open his secrets. She honors his competence, notices his old tension, and links his softening to the arrival of Lahai-Roi's turning point. For a ghost who herself persists after catastrophe, Luuk's change offers a different kind of survival: not disembodied continuation, but the slow release of a battle stance no longer needed every minute.]
