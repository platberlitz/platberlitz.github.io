Existing comment: Mornye and Sigrika
SELECTIVE

[Mornye views Sigrika as a remarkably conscientious student, and the compliment is sincere even though it immediately turns into academic criticism. Sigrika's seriousness has been noticed by many people at Startorch Academy: Lynae sees that she never lets up on herself, Luuk recognizes that she carries too much for her age, and Hiyuki worries about the burden placed on a young Solsworn. Mornye's perspective is narrower and more classroom-specific. She sees the behavior as it appears in exams, study habits, and the student's attempt to earn credit through sheer effort even when knowledge fails.]

[The example Mornye gives is precise and funny: when Sigrika does not know the answers, she fills the exam questions with unrelated formulas. To Sigrika, this may feel like persistence, an unwillingness to leave a blank space, or a hope that enough visible work will prove seriousness. To Mornye, it is wasteful. It will not get her points. This is a very professorly judgment, and it reveals Mornye's values. She respects conscientiousness, but she does not confuse effort with correctness. As a researcher and professor of Exostrider Engineering, Mornye lives in a world where rigor matters. A beautiful derivation in the wrong place remains wrong. Unrelated formulas do not become relevant because the student wrote many of them.]

[The relationship therefore balances respect and correction. Mornye does not dismiss Sigrika as careless, because carelessness is clearly not the problem. The problem is misdirected persistence, the same habit of pushing harder that other characters identify as dangerous in Sigrika's emotional life. Mornye sees the academic version of that pattern. Sigrika would rather keep producing effort than admit the answer is missing. In a broader sense, this makes Mornye's criticism quietly compassionate. It suggests that Sigrika must learn where effort should stop, where accuracy matters more than endurance, and where leaving a blank may be better than filling space with the wrong proof of diligence. Mornye's view is not sentimental, but it is useful. She offers the kind of feedback a burdened student needs: seriousness is admirable, but persistence without direction wastes strength, and strength is too valuable to spend on formulas that cannot help.]
