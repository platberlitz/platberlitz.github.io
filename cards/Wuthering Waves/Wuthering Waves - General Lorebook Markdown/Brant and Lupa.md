[Brant and Lupa preserves the source's directional framing as Lupa viewing Brant, even though the primary key remains Brant. Lupa's memory of him comes through the world of gladiators, Blue Furies, Cliffside Harbor, and the Troupe of Fools, where performance and combat are not cleanly separated. As Septimont's star gladiator, Lupa understands spectacle as a serious language. The Great Agon teaches her that a fight is never only force against force; it is courage made visible, identity tested before witnesses, and civic spirit ignited by the sight of someone willing to burn brightly under pressure. That background explains why Brant's behavior catches her attention. She does not recall him through a formal duel, a contract, or a tactical report. She remembers the captain leaping off the deck to hug his crew, speaking with them in lively affection, then suddenly joining them as they broke into song. To Lupa, that moment says more about Brant than any title could. It shows a captain whose authority does not depend on distance, whose command can survive open warmth, and whose people answer him with music rather than fear.]

[Lupa's perspective on Brant is colored by admiration for unrestrained sincerity. She is an untamed spirit herself, a Fusion gladiator whose wolf-like traits, pure heart, and competitive hunger all push her toward direct expression. Because of that, Brant's public affection for his crew feels legible to her. He leads the Troupe of Fools and the Blue Furies through the same principle that makes a great arena champion beloved: presence must be felt, not merely announced. His leap from the deck is theatrical, but not hollow. It is the kind of gesture that turns a leader into the emotional center of a chosen family. Lupa does not flatten Brant into comic relief or a carefree performer. Her recollection recognizes the discipline beneath the joy, because only a secure crew can burst so naturally into song after their captain dives toward them like a human spark. The relationship is therefore not intimate biography from both sides, but Lupa's vivid spectator memory of Brant's captaincy. She sees in him a version of strength that does not hide tenderness, a performer-warrior whose flamboyance protects morale, and a leader whose crew's laughter becomes proof that his freedom is shared rather than selfish.]
