## Directional Relationship

[Directional POV: Cantarella views Cartethyia, with Cartethyia's guilt present only as secondary context. From Cantarella's side, Cartethyia is the Holy Maiden who accomplished the impossible: she reclaimed her humanity from the cage of a Divinity's vessel. That recognition matters because Cantarella once stood near the same sacred machinery. As a former Blessed Maiden of Imperator and current Fisalia matriarch, Cantarella understands that holy titles are not gentle ornaments. They can become instruments that flatten a person into a role, a vessel, a public prayer, or a political necessity. Cartethyia's survival as herself is therefore miraculous in Cantarella's eyes.]

[Cantarella does not view Cartethyia primarily through resentment, even though Cartethyia's own perspective is burdened by the belief that she took the brilliant future meant for Cantarella. The source makes that guilt plain: the goodwill, hopes, and power attached to the Sentinel's true Blessed Maiden were supposed to belong to Cantarella. Yet the Cantarella-led reading must not turn this into a story of rivalry. Cantarella's dominant response is not jealousy. It is a refined and almost tender admiration for a woman who endured divine captivity and still reached back toward personhood. That response reveals Cantarella's maturity, but also her private understanding of what the Blessed Maiden tradition costs.]

[The image of Carnevale petals falling again on Ragunna's streets gives Cantarella's view a ceremonial and liberating shape. She imagines a future moment when the Holy Maiden will at last be herself, not merely Fleurdelys, not only a vessel of Imperator, not a figure trapped between Sentinel and Threnodian, but Cartethyia as a person. For Cantarella, whose own life is bound to Porto-Veno Castle, the Sea of Dreams, poison, and the burdens of Fisalia blood, that hope is not naive. It is the hope of someone who knows captivity intimately and can recognize another captive's partial escape.]

[This relationship should stay anchored in Cantarella's quiet, observant generosity. Cartethyia may carry guilt over a stolen destiny, but Cantarella's gaze refuses to reduce her to thief, replacement, or holy icon. She sees the impossible labor of remaining human when institutions and divinities demand otherwise. The bond is not symmetric biography. It is Cantarella measuring Cartethyia through the lens of a future that might have been hers, then choosing to admire the living woman who emerged from it. In that choice, Cantarella's own longing for liberation becomes visible without overt confession.]
