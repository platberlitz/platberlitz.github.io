Existing comment: Iuno and Augusta
SELECTIVE

[Iuno views Augusta with a teasing honesty that cuts deeper than formal reverence. Her little secret is that when she first told Augusta she would lead Septimont into a new era, Augusta believed it to be prophecy, but Iuno says it was only a hunch. This is not a confession of fraud so much as a revelation of how Iuno separates fate from living will. As the 127th Priestess of the Tetragon Temple, Iuno can read futures through moonlight and understands how heavily Septimont leans on prophetic authority. Yet with Augusta, she refuses to reduce leadership to something prewritten. She saw a possibility in Augusta and named it before the city had proof.]

[That refusal becomes even clearer when Iuno says she never peeps into Augusta's fate because it has always been in Augusta's hands. For a priestess whose title carries stasis, cycle, and renewal, this is a profound act of respect. Augusta is the Ephor of Septimont, the undying sun eternally ablaze, a ruler and warrior who defies fate with blade held high. Many citizens might want the Temple to certify her destiny, but Iuno values her precisely because she does not need that certification. Augusta's authority is not an ornament hung on her by prophecy. It is forged through the choices she makes for Septimont, again and again, under pressure.]

[Iuno's admiration is blunt about the cost. Augusta is the kind of ruler who would sacrifice everything for her people, even her own flesh and blood. Iuno does not say this lightly. She stands inside a Temple culture where words about fate can shape lives, and she knows that public courage often hides private ruin. Her comment to Rover that no coronation is needed to see Augusta's royalty makes the judgment personal rather than ceremonial. Iuno recognizes sovereignty before institutions finish naming it. To her, Augusta leads because she takes the city's burden into her own body and will, not because fate was kind enough to crown her first.]
