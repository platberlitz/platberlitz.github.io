# Aemeath and Hiyuki

[This entry preserves Hiyuki's directional view of Aemeath, and it is marked by restrained empathy. Hiyuki calls Aemeath a resilient child and concludes that she must have endured unimaginable hardship to return. The phrasing is severe but not cold. Hiyuki is a long-serving miko, Special Response Force operative, and bearer of sacred burdens tied to Ashinohara, Lahai-Roi, and difficult possibilities. She does not sentimentalize suffering, which makes her recognition of Aemeath's endurance feel especially weighty. To Hiyuki, Aemeath is not merely a casualty of an Academy project. She is someone who crossed pain severe enough to make return itself remarkable.]

[Hiyuki's account is also shaped by absence. When Aemeath vanished within the simulator cockpit, Hiyuki was away on a field assignment. That detail matters because it frames her knowledge as delayed, mediated through the investigation team rather than witnessed first-hand. Hiyuki is accustomed to duty pulling people away from personal moments, and here duty kept her from the crisis as it happened. Her later attention to the case suggests a quiet sense of responsibility, or at least the seriousness with which she receives reports involving students, Resonators, and extraordinary conviction. She could not be present when Aemeath disappeared, but she did not treat the report as disposable.]

[The detail that makes Hiyuki pause is Aemeath's childhood or student aspiration: saving the world. Hiyuki wonders what in Aemeath's upbringing instilled such conviction. This question reveals more about Hiyuki's perspective than any direct praise could. As someone driven by entrusted wishes, long service, and the cost of confronting abnormal threats, Hiyuki understands that a phrase like saving the world can be childish, sacred, naive, or terrifying depending on the life behind it. In Aemeath's case, the aspiration appears to have become real enough to shape fatal action. The girl who wanted to save the world became a Synchronist who overclocked to resonate with Exostrider and survived only as a digital ghost.]

[The dossier should remain directional because Hiyuki's view places Aemeath within a moral field of duty, hardship, and unexplained conviction. She does not know every intimate detail of Aemeath's upbringing, and the source preserves her wondering rather than answering the question. That restraint is important. Hiyuki sees a child who endured too much, notices the dream that preceded the catastrophe, and lets the mystery remain. In her eyes, Aemeath's tragedy is not only that she vanished in a cockpit. It is that someone so young had already learned to aim herself at saving the world, and that the world accepted the cost.]
