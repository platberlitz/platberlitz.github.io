Existing comment: Iuno and Lupa
SELECTIVE

[Iuno's view of Lupa centers on the purity of competition. Although the source entry later preserves fragments of Lupa's opinion of Iuno, the directional heart of this dossier is Iuno watching Lupa and finding her passion remarkable. Lupa is Septimont's star gladiator, the Howling Flame, a champion whose name belongs to the Great Agon and whose Fusion fire turns the arena into spectacle. Iuno does not begin by analyzing her title, noble history, undefeated record, or political usefulness. She begins with the simple truth that Lupa loves the fight. For a priestess surrounded by prophecies, omens, expectations, and the heavy language of destiny, such directness is refreshing.]

[Iuno says that for Lupa, victory and glory are only icing on the cake compared to the joy of the fight. This is a subtle but important distinction. Septimont's arena culture can turn combat into civic status, noble influence, public myth, and proof of worth. Lupa certainly receives glory from it, but Iuno sees that glory is not the root of her fire. The root is participation itself: the chance to test her body, spirit, instincts, and courage against a worthy challenge. Lupa's lack of fear toward losing deepens Iuno's respect. Someone obsessed only with victory would be ruled by fate's result. Lupa is ruled by the act of giving everything before the result arrives.]

[That difference speaks directly to Iuno's own rebellion against deterministic cruelty. As a Temple Priestess, she knows how easily people can live as if the ending has already swallowed the struggle. Lupa's passion insists otherwise. No matter the odds, she gives her all and refuses to back down, not because prophecy guarantees success, but because the fight itself deserves full presence. Iuno admires that kind of freedom. Lupa becomes, in Iuno's eyes, a living argument against passive fate: a wolf-flamed gladiator who proves that joy, effort, and courage still matter even when defeat remains possible.]
