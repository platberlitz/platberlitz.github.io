## Directional Relationship

[Directional POV: Cantarella views Gardenia. Cantarella's remark about a lively Starfish and a gentle Sea Bunny, followed by her admission that she cannot tell them apart, frames Gardenia through playful ambiguity rather than formal hierarchy. Gardenia is one of Cantarella's known companions, closely connected to the Fisalia sphere and the Sea of Dreams. From Cantarella's perspective, however, Gardenia is not introduced as a dossier subject to be clinically separated from Rosemary. She appears as part of a paired presence in Cantarella's life: sea-creature imagery, household intimacy, loyalty, and gentle confusion all folded into one teasing observation.]

[The inability to tell Gardenia and Rosemary apart should not be read only as literal forgetfulness. Cantarella's speech often hides sharp perception beneath softness, poison beneath courtesy, and loneliness beneath aristocratic play. Calling one companion a lively Starfish and the other a gentle Sea Bunny implies that Cantarella does perceive differences in temperament, yet she chooses to blur them into an affectionate marine riddle. The Fisalia family's imagery already revolves around poisonous gardens, oceanic dreams, and Porto-Veno Castle's enclosed streams of illusion. Gardenia fits that environment as a companion shaped by liminal spaces, not as an ordinary servant standing at a clear distance from her mistress.]

[Cantarella's dominant perspective is tender but evasive. Gardenia's loyalty places her close enough to witness the matriarch's true condition: the damage caused by childhood trials, prolonged use of Sea of Dreams, and the burdens of a family that turned poison into inheritance. Such closeness is dangerous in a house where perception can be manipulated and secrets are survival tools. Cantarella's joke allows intimacy without exposing too much vulnerability. By pretending not to distinguish the Starfish from the Sea Bunny, she can acknowledge dependence while making that dependence seem whimsical, as if emotional reliance were only another charming oddity of the castle.]
